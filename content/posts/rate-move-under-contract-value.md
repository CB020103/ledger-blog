Here's a scenario worth thinking through carefully, because it's playing out on a deal I'm watching right now, and it's a clean illustration of a risk that's easy to describe in theory but more interesting to actually trace through in practice. A multifamily deal gets awarded, priced off agency debt tied to the 7-year Treasury. Since award, the 7-year has moved up about 20 basis points, and the deal is still sitting under contract, debt not yet locked, closing not yet happened. What does that actually do to the deal?

## Why a rate move hits value, not just the rate

The instinct is to think a higher rate just means a slightly worse loan for the borrower. That undersells what's actually happening. Most agency and permanent multifamily debt gets sized to whichever constraint binds harder: a maximum loan-to-value ratio, or a minimum debt service coverage ratio (DSCR). On a lot of stabilized multifamily deals today, especially anything priced at a tight cap rate, the DSCR constraint is the binding one, not the LTV constraint.

A rate increase doesn't touch the LTV side of that equation at all. It goes straight at DSCR. The same in-place NOI now has to cover a larger annual debt service payment at the higher rate, which means the maximum loan amount that still clears the lender's minimum DSCR floor gets smaller. Same property, same income, smaller loan.

## Where that pressure actually lands

A smaller loan on an unchanged purchase price means the buyer has to bring more equity to close the same deal. Equity is the more expensive form of capital in the stack, so replacing debt with more equity, at the same purchase price, degrades the return the buyer originally underwrote. The typical response to that isn't to just accept a worse return. It's to go back to the seller and ask for a lower purchase price, enough to restore the return that was underwritten at award.

That's the actual transmission mechanism from a rate move to a value move. It isn't abstract. It runs through loan sizing, then through required equity, then through the return math, and it lands on price.

## The real lesson: basis risk lives in the gap between award and close

The part of this that's worth internalizing is that the risk isn't really about rates in general, it's about the specific window between when a deal gets awarded and when the debt actually gets locked and funded. That gap can run 60 to 90 days on a lot of deals, and during that window, the buyer is fully exposed to whatever the market does with rates, unless there's a hedge in place.

This is exactly why forward rate locks and Treasury locks exist as real negotiating points in agency financing. Locking the rate at award, rather than letting it float until closing, is how a buyer removes this exact risk from the table. A 20bp move over a 60 to 90 day contract period isn't a huge swing in absolute terms, but it's a live, current example of a risk that's easy to gloss over in a term sheet and much more real once you're actually watching a deal sit under contract while the market moves underneath it.
