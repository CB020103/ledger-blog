# Cap Rates Aren't as Simple as They Sound (Here's What Actually Confused Me)

## The Formula Is Easy. The Language Is Not.

**Cap Rate = NOI / Price**

For a fixed NOI, a higher cap rate means a lower price. That part is straightforward Ã¢â‚¬â€ you're paying less for each dollar of income, so the deal is "cheaper" in that sense.

Where it gets confusing is when people say things like "buyers are willing to pay for a 6 cap." That phrase sounds like it should mean a bigger, richer number is better. But flip it around: what they actually mean is *"buyers will only price this deal so that it works out to a 6% return."* It's shorthand for the price a buyer will set, not a description of a premium.

So a market where buyers only accept a 6 cap is actually a more buyer-friendly, cheaper market than one where buyers are competing hard enough to accept a 5 cap or lower. Lower accepted cap rate = buyers paying more per dollar of income = a hotter, more expensive market.

## Why a Tax Abatement Changes the Cap Rate (Without Changing the Price)

A common example: a deal gets pitched as "6.5 cap with the tax abatement in place, more like 5 if not."

The abatement doesn't change the purchase price Ã¢â‚¬â€ it changes the NOI. Lower property taxes mean higher net income, and since price is fixed, a higher NOI produces a higher cap rate. Same building, same price, two different tax scenarios, two different returns.

The catch: abatements expire. So that headline 6.5 cap is often a going-in number that steps down once the abatement burns off and property taxes reassess. Anyone underwriting this kind of deal needs to model the post-abatement NOI, not just the number being marketed today.

## Exit Cap Rates: What Happens When You Sell

Your going-in cap rate is locked the day you buy Ã¢â‚¬â€ it's simply the return based on the price you paid. During your hold, you're not really "increasing your cap rate." You're growing NOI (higher rents, better expense management, lease-up, etc.). That improvement shows up as a rising **yield on cost** Ã¢â‚¬â€ your return relative to your original basis.

But when you sell, the buyer isn't pricing off your yield on cost. They're pricing off *their* required return applied to the property's current NOI:

**Sale Price = Exit-Year NOI / Exit Cap Rate**

The exit cap rate is whatever return buyers are demanding in the market *at the time you sell* Ã¢â‚¬â€ a fresh, independent number that has nothing to do with what you originally paid.

This is why "selling at a lower exit cap" is the good outcome. A lower cap rate means a higher price for the same dollar of income Ã¢â‚¬â€ often called **cap rate compression**. If it happens on top of NOI growth, you get paid twice: once for improving the property, and again because the market is now willing to pay more for every dollar of income it produces.

The opposite, **cap rate expansion**, is the risk that eats returns even when the property performed well operationally Ã¢â‚¬â€ buyers demand a higher return, so the sale price compresses regardless of your NOI gains. This is exactly why conservative underwriting typically assumes the exit cap rate will be equal to or higher than the entry cap rate: it's a hedge that doesn't rely on market generosity.

- **Core+** Ã¢â‚¬â€ a bit more risk: some lease rollover, modest upside, secondary market, maybe more leverage. Slightly higher cap rate to compensate.
- **Value-add** Ã¢â‚¬â€ real renovation, releasing, or repositioning needed. More execution risk, meaningfully higher cap rates.

## What a 4 Cap (or Lower) Usually Means

A very low cap rate is worth pausing on, because it can mean a few very different things:

1. **Ultra-core, trophy asset.** Best-in-class, irreplaceable location, deep buyer pool. Risk is about as low as real estate gets, so required return is low.

3. **Broader rate environment.** When required returns compress across the board (like the near-zero rate era of 2020Ã¢â‚¬â€œ2021), even ordinary assets can trade at 4-ish caps for macro reasons that have little to do with the specific property.
4. **Aggressive underwriting.** A 4 cap can also mean a buyer is pricing in growth that may not show up, or riding cheap capital and betting heavily on appreciation with very little current-yield cushion if things don't go to plan.

The gut check: is the low cap rate justified by genuinely low risk, or is it a bet on growth that hasn't happened yet? Both can produce the same number on paper, but one is a defensive, bond-like position and the other is a growth bet Ã¢â‚¬â€ and the lower the going-in yield, the less room there is for anything to go wrong before the deal underperforms.
