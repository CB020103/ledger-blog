title: "The Strait of Hormuz Isn't Just an Oil Story, It's a CRE Story"

After a call with a partner at TPG, I found myself thinking more about how macro trends actually filter down into the asset classes I care about, and for me, that's real estate. It's easy to read Strait of Hormuz headlines and think about gas prices at the pump. Fewer people are thinking about cap rates. But the transmission from a Middle East energy shock to a Charlotte multifamily deal is more direct than it looks.

## 1. Construction costs move first

Asphalt, PVC, insulation, and fuel for equipment and material transport are all petroleum linked. A sustained oil spike doesn't just raise gas prices at the pump, it raises the cost of literally building or renovating a property, compressing development margins and pushing some projects from "feasible" to "shelved."

## 2. Operating costs follow

Utilities, especially natural gas and electricity generation tied to it, are a real line item in NOI, particularly for energy intensive property types like industrial and hospitality. A sustained energy shock doesn't show up in headlines about your local apartment complex, but it shows up in the expense line of the T-12.

## 3. The bigger channel is monetary policy, not the direct cost line

Oil shocks are inflationary. Inflation pushes the Fed toward higher for longer rates. Higher rates raise the cost of debt and push cap rates out, which compresses valuations across the board. This is usually a bigger valuation lever than the direct cost impact on any single property.

## 4. Some sectors feel it more directly than others

Industrial and logistics assets near ports are exposed to shipping cost volatility. Gulf Coast markets with energy sector employment (Houston being the clearest example) see office and multifamily demand tied to oil company hiring and capex cycles. Hospitality is sensitive to travel costs, which move with jet fuel prices.

## The takeaway

You don't need to be a geopolitics expert to underwrite real estate, but ignoring events like this means missing a real input into your NOI growth assumptions, your cap rate expectations, and your read on which markets are more or less exposed. Macro isn't background noise for CRE, it's an input.
