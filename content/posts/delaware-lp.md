Today's observation: a property with two towers, where each tower is
set up as its own separate legal entity - specifically, a **Delaware
Limited Partnership (LP)**. Wanted to understand why you'd bother
splitting one property into multiple entities like that.

## GP and LP, the core structure

A Delaware LP has two kinds of partners:

- **General Partner (GP)** - runs the show. Full control over
  management and decisions, but also carries full liability for the
  partnership's debts and obligations.
- **Limited Partner (LP)** - puts up capital, gets a share of the
  income, but stays out of day-to-day management. In exchange for
  staying passive, an LP's liability is capped at what they invested -
  their personal assets aren't reachable.

This maps directly onto how most real estate deals are actually
capitalized: a sponsor (the GP) manages the deal, and outside investors
(the LPs) provide most of the capital passively. It's exactly the
structure behind most syndications and institutional real estate
deals.

## Why split each tower into its own LP

The idea of using a **separate entity per asset** isn't unique to LPs -
you see the same logic with single-purpose LLCs too. The reasoning:

- **Liability isolation.** If something goes wrong at Tower A (a
  lawsuit, a default), that exposure stays contained within Tower A's
  entity. Tower B, sitting in its own separate partnership, isn't
  exposed.
- **Financing flexibility.** Lenders generally want to lend against one
  clean, isolated asset - not a bundle of unrelated properties. A
  separate entity per tower means each one can be financed, refinanced,
  or sold independently without untangling it from the other.
- **Different capital structures per asset.** If one tower has a
  different capital partner, a different ownership split, or different
  economics than the other, keeping them as separate LPs makes that
  clean and contractually simple, rather than forcing one messy
  ownership structure across both.

## Why Delaware specifically

Real estate uses Delaware for the entity itself for pretty practical
reasons: flexible, contract-driven partnership agreements, a well-
established business court (the Court of Chancery) that gives lenders
and investors legal predictability, and privacy - the partners' names
don't have to show up on the public formation filing the way they
might elsewhere.

## The part I hadn't considered

The GP/LP split isn't just a liability trick - it's also the natural
container for the actual economics of a deal: the GP earns fees and a
promote for managing and executing the business plan, while LPs earn
a preferred return and their share of the profit for providing the
capital. Splitting a two-tower property into separate LPs means that
same GP/LP economic structure can be applied cleanly to each tower,
instead of getting muddled across a single combined entity.
