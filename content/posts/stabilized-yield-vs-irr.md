## Two ways to answer the same question

A conversation with someone on a real estate private equity team got me thinking harder about a metric I don't use nearly as often as IRR: stabilized yield. It is a simple ratio, stabilized NOI divided by either total cost or purchase price, and it tells you the going-in or exit yield on a property once it has reached steady-state occupancy and rents. No discounting, no time value of money, just a clean snapshot of what the asset produces relative to what it took to get there.

IRR, by contrast, is the metric most of us default to first. It captures the full path of cash flows over a hold period and compresses everything, timing included, into a single annualized percentage. That is exactly what makes it useful, and exactly what makes it easy to misread.

## Where IRR can mislead

IRR is a time-weighted, annualized number, which means it rewards speed as much as it rewards profit. A small check returned quickly can post an enormous IRR even though the actual dollars made were modest. A $50,000 profit on a $200,000 investment held for six months annualizes into a headline-grabbing return that does not really tell you much about the deal's quality.

There is also the reinvestment assumption baked into the math. IRR implicitly assumes that every interim cash flow gets reinvested at that same IRR, which is rarely realistic in practice. And because it is a relative metric, it says nothing about scale. An 18% IRR on a $2 million profit and an 18% IRR on a $200,000 profit look identical on paper, even though one deal moved a lot more capital.

IRR can also be shaped by the timing and structure of cash flows themselves. Front-loading a distribution or delaying a capital call can shift the IRR meaningfully without changing the underlying economics of the asset at all.

## Why stabilized yield holds up

Stabilized yield sidesteps most of that. It is not path-dependent, so it does not care how the deal was financed, sequenced, or timed. It is a direct read on the asset's fundamentals once it is performing at a normalized level, which makes it a cleaner apples-to-apples comparison across deals, especially for investors underwriting many deals across different hold periods and capital structures.

That does not make it a replacement for IRR. It answers a different question. IRR tells you about the return on your capital over time. Stabilized yield tells you about the asset itself, independent of how you got there. The best underwriting probably wants both in the room, not one instead of the other, but it makes sense why an investor doing high-volume deal review might lean on stabilized yield as a faster first filter before digging into the full return profile.
