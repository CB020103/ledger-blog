## Wait, competitive Excel is a real sport?

Sort of. There's an organization called the Financial Modeling World Cup
(FMWC) that runs a full season of Excel and modeling competitions, ending
in an in-person finals in Las Vegas. One track is the Financial Modeling
World Cup itself — real valuation and modeling case studies. The other is
the Microsoft Excel World Championship (MEWC), which strips out the finance
entirely and just tests raw Excel and logical thinking under a clock.

Both run on a genuinely competitive structure: qualifying rounds, brackets,
a prize fund, and a live finals with an actual arena setup — commentary,
jerseys, the whole bit.

## Meet the reigning champion

The current title-holder is **Diarmuid Early**, from Galway, Ireland, who
dethroned Ngai to win the 2025 Microsoft Excel World Championship. His
route into competitive Excel is arguably the most "finance career" of
anyone in the scene: he holds a doctorate in computer science, spent about
ten years as a business manager at Deutsche Bank, and picked up serious
Excel modeling experience during a stint at Boston Consulting Group back
in 2008. He's also a two-time World Financial Modeling Champion and racked
up several runner-up finishes before finally taking the top spot.

These days he's gone independent — he runs his own firm, Early Days
Consulting, and partners at another modeling-focused practice, doing
financial modeling, reporting, and automation work for clients. He's also
become one of the sport's main educators, regularly posting full
case-solving walkthroughs so people can actually see how a top competitor
thinks through a problem in real time.

What I like about his path specifically: it's not "borderline pro gamer who
also does finance," it's "a genuinely senior person from a bank and a top
consulting firm who happens to also be world-class at the tool everyone in
finance uses every day." That's a pretty strong argument that the skill
being tested here isn't a novelty — it's the same muscle used in real jobs,
just measured under a clock.

## And the guy he dethroned

**Andrew Ngai** is a Sydney-based actuary who competes under the nickname
"the Annihilator." He's won the Microsoft Excel
World Championship three years in a row, plus the Financial Modeling World
Cup title back in 2019. He's not some random hobbyist either — he's spent
over a decade using Excel daily in his actual job, first as a consultant at
PwC, then at Willis Towers Watson, and now as a director at an actuarial
firm called Taylor Fry.

What's interesting is his own explanation for why he's good at it: not some
secret trick, just a habit of refusing to keep using a messy or inefficient
spreadsheet and rebuilding it properly instead. That's a pretty good one-
line philosophy for anyone learning modeling, honestly.

## So why does this matter for a finance career

Here's the part that makes it more than a novelty: the competitions are
explicitly positioned as a recruiting funnel, not just a hobby event.

- There's a dedicated free track just for students, designed so they can
  build their resume and get noticed by companies while still in school
- The organization runs paid services for firms — financial assessment
  tests and tailored tournaments — specifically so companies can scout
  talent through the competition
- Multiple competitors have reported landing interviews, consulting work,
  and even full job offers directly off the back of a strong showing

It's essentially a live, public skills test that doubles as a resume line —
which is a pretty unusual thing to exist in finance recruiting, where most
signal comes from GPA, network, or where you interned.

## The part that's actually relevant to us

This is basically the same instinct behind building this blog: instead of
just claiming you know DCFs or CRE modeling, you show a body of real,
checkable work. FMWC's whole existence is proof that finance firms *want*
that kind of demonstrable skill signal — they built an entire competition
around sourcing it.

Worth keeping an eye on. The case studies and sample problems are public,
