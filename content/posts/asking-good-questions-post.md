I noticed the same thing twice in one day, from two completely different
settings, and it stuck with me.

First, I sat in on a managing director's meeting where she was checking in
with a senior associate on a project he's been leading. I won't get into
the specifics of what it was about, but what actually struck me wasn't
the project itself, it was how she asked about it. Sharp, specific
follow-up questions, delivered with zero hesitation, like asking wasn't
something she had to work up to. It was just how she operated.

Later that same day, I had a networking call with a Princeton alum who's
very senior at TPG. I was asking him for career and interview advice, and
he ended up walking me through the kinds of questions I should be asking
in interviews, and the kinds of questions I might get asked about real
estate markets. What got me wasn't just the content, it was that he's on
the credit side, not real estate directly, and he still had sharp,
specific things to say. He clearly asks good questions of the people
around him constantly, which is probably exactly how he built that range
of knowledge in the first place.

Two totally different people, two totally different contexts, same
underlying skill on display.

## Why this feels like the actual differentiator

It's easy to assume what separates senior, successful people from
everyone else is some deep reservoir of answers, like they've simply
accumulated more knowledge than the rest of us. But both of these
moments looked more like the opposite. It wasn't that they already knew
everything. It was that they were still actively, confidently asking,
long after they'd earned the right to just assume things or stay quiet.

I think a lot of people, especially early in a career, quietly worry
that asking a question signals they don't already know the answer. What
I saw this week was pretty much the reverse: the more senior someone
gets, the more comfortable, and more skilled, they seem to be at asking.
Not despite their seniority, but almost because of it.

## The actual takeaway

Confidence in asking questions might be one of the most underrated
leadership traits there is, and it's genuinely learnable starting right
now, not something to wait on until I feel "senior enough" to ask
without embarrassment. If the people at the very top are still doing it
constantly and well, that's a pretty good sign it's worth practicing
early, not something you eventually grow out of needing.
