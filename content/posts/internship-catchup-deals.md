A few weeks into my internship now, and I wanted to catch this site up on
the deal-side concepts that have actually started to click. I'm keeping
this to the general skills and frameworks I've picked up — not the
specifics of any live deal, since that's obviously not mine to share.

## Reading the deal, not just the numbers

Early on, most of my time went into reading Offering Memorandums (OMs)
and sitting in on buyer interviews. Two things stood out:

- **An OM is a sales pitch with data behind it** — learning to read one
  means separating the narrative (why this deal is great) from the
  underlying numbers (rent roll, expenses, comps) that actually support
  or undercut that narrative.
- **Buyer interviews vary enormously** depending on how prepared and
  experienced the buyer is. Some come in with a sharp, specific thesis
  and clear numbers; others are still feeling out the market. Watching
  that range taught me a lot about what "knowing a market" actually
  looks like in practice, versus just having capital to deploy.

## Comps: rent vs. sales, and where CoStar fits in

I hadn't appreciated how different these two types of comps really are:

- **Rent comps** tell you what similar units are actually leasing for
  right now — the input for your income assumptions.
- **Sales comps** tell you what similar properties have traded for — the
  input for your exit and valuation assumptions.

Learning to actually pull and evaluate both of these using CoStar, rather
than just knowing the definitions, was its own skill — deciding what
counts as "comparable" takes judgment, not just a filter.

## The debt side: DSCR, debt yield, and LTV

Got a proper primer on how lenders actually size a loan, which was new
to me. The core idea: a lender runs **three separate tests** —

- **DSCR test** (Debt Service Coverage Ratio) — can the property's income
  cover the debt payment with room to spare?
- **Debt yield test** — NOI divided by loan amount, a lender's simple
  sanity check independent of interest rate
- **LTV test** (Loan-to-Value) — how much of the purchase price the loan
  actually covers

The part that surprised me: the lender doesn't just average these or
pick their favorite. **They calculate the loan amount under all three
tests and take the smallest one.** The most conservative test wins,
every time.

## Building a buyer offer matrix

Watched (and eventually helped build) an offer matrix — basically a
structured way to lay every incoming Letter of Intent (LOI) side by side:
price, terms, closing timeline, financing contingencies, buyer track
record. The value isn't just comparing headline price — a lower offer
with cleaner terms and a more credible buyer can beat a higher offer
that's shakier.

## Refi vs. recap

Learned the actual distinction here, which I'd been fuzzy on:

- **Refinance (refi)** — replacing existing debt with new debt, same
  ownership structure
- **Recapitalization (recap)** — restructuring the ownership/equity
  side of a deal, which may or may not involve new debt too

## Tax abatements

Sat in on a discussion of how a state-level tax abatement gets factored
into underwriting — this ties directly into something I wrote about
separately (see my cap rates post): an abatement inflates the going-in
NOI and cap rate without changing the underlying value, and a
sophisticated buyer has to model the post-abatement numbers, not just
the marketed headline.
