I did not expect to be writing a check-in post on my own theses this soon, but this week is doing it for me. Oil has pushed past $100 a barrel after attacks on tankers in the Red Sea and an escalation in US-Iran hostilities, on pace for one of its largest monthly gains in years. The 10-year Treasury yield has followed it up to around 4.64%, a two-month high, as the oil spike revives inflation fears. The Fed is still expected to hold rates steady at its meeting this week, but futures markets are now pricing something close to a coin flip on a hike in September. That is a real reversal from where rate-cut expectations sat earlier this year.

## This is the Hormuz thesis showing up in real time

A few weeks ago I wrote about how a Middle East energy shock filters into CRE through construction costs, operating costs, and financing costs, not just gas prices at the pump. That was a framework built from first principles. This week is the framework happening live. Oil past $100 is not a hypothetical input to a construction cost model anymore, it is the actual number underwriters are staring at right now.

## This is also the rate-move-under-contract post, at scale

I also wrote recently about a single deal I was watching where the 7-year Treasury moved 20 basis points between award and close, and traced how that pressures DSCR-constrained loan sizing and ultimately property value. The 10-year just moved considerably more than that in a matter of days, driven by the same oil shock. Every deal sitting under contract on a Treasury-indexed rate lock right now is living through a much larger version of that exact mechanism, all at once, across the entire market rather than one property.

## The AI capex thread gets a reality check too

Separately, and almost unrelated on the surface, Alphabet dropped roughly 7% this week despite beating earnings, purely because it raised its AI capital spending guidance. That is a live data point for the AI infrastructure post I wrote about China's data center buildout and the tension between falling per-token costs and rising total compute demand. Investors reacting negatively to more capex, even attached to strong underlying results, is the market asking the exact question I raised: is this spending going to show up as durable earnings, or is some of it going to end up looking like the overbuilt, underutilized capacity that shows up in some of China's own data center numbers.

## Why this is worth sitting with, not just noting

None of these three threads are new ideas this week. What is new is watching them all move at once, in the same direction, off the same underlying shock. Oil geopolitics, rate risk on deals under contract, and skepticism about AI infrastructure spending are usually written about as separate stories. This week they are the same story, and it is a useful reminder that the frameworks are only as good as their ability to hold up when the inputs actually move, not just when I am building them in the abstract.
