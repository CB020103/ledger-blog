Today had three separate storylines that all matter for CRE, and the tricky part is they don't all point the same direction.

## 1. Wells Fargo's quarter shows what "free capital" actually looks like

Wells Fargo posted Q2 net income of $6.4 billion, or $2.00 per diluted share, blowing past the $1.72 analyst estimate. Revenue rose 9% to $22.6 billion. Management was explicit about why: this is the first full year operating without the asset cap the Fed imposed back in 2018 and removed in 2025, and the bank is now free to actually grow its balance sheet instead of just shuffling it. Average loans were up 12% year over year. For CRE specifically, that matters because Wells has historically been one of the largest bank lenders to the sector, so a bank with newly unconstrained balance sheet capacity and strong credit performance is a real signal for debt availability going forward, not just a bank-stock story.

## 2. CPI actually fell, which nobody was expecting

June CPI dropped 0.4% month over month, the largest one-month decline since 2020, versus economist forecasts for a much smaller 0.1% decline. Core CPI (excluding food and energy) was flat on the month. The headline drop was driven mostly by a sharp fall in energy prices. On its face, that's disinflationary and good news for the rate outlook.

Worth being precise here: there was no Fed meeting today. The FOMC's last decision was June 17, holding the target range at 3.50% to 3.75%, and the next meeting isn't until July 28 and 29. What today's CPI print actually did was reduce the market-implied odds of a hike at that upcoming meeting, not produce a new rate decision.

## 3. But Treasury yields climbed anyway, and that's the real tension

Here's the part that doesn't fit the clean "soft CPI, good news" narrative: the 10-year Treasury is sitting around 4.60%, near its highest level in about two months. That's not the CPI report's doing, it's the same Strait of Hormuz situation I wrote about in my last macro post. Renewed conflict and a reinstated blockade have pushed oil prices up, and that's keeping a geopolitical risk premium baked into long-end yields even as the domestic inflation data softens.

## What this means for CRE

This is the split that actually matters for underwriting: CPI is telling you domestic demand-side inflation is cooling, which is a tailwind for the Fed eventually easing off a hike. But the 10-year, which cap rates track more directly than the fed funds rate, is being held up by an external, oil-driven risk premium that has nothing to do with the domestic economy. That's a genuinely different setup than "inflation is falling, therefore cap rates will compress." Until the geopolitical premium unwinds, borrowing costs stay elevated regardless of what CPI does, which argues for underwriting debt costs conservatively even in the face of good inflation prints. Meanwhile, a bank like Wells Fargo lending more freely is a longer-term tailwind for capital availability, but it doesn't do anything to fix the near-term cost of that capital.
