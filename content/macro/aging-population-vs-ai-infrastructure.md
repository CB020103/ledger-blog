Every capital allocation conversation I've had this year eventually turns to AI. Data centers, power demand, the infrastructure buildout, all of it. It's a real trend, but it's also a trend that's starting to show cracks: development pipelines in several markets are already outrunning near-term absorption, which is exactly the setup that turns a hot trend into an oversupply story.

There's a quieter trend sitting right next to it that I think is a better place to actually deploy capital in 2026: senior housing.

## 1. The demand side isn't a forecast, it's arithmetic

The oldest baby boomers turn 80 this year. That's the age senior housing usage really starts to climb, and it's not a projection or a survey result, it's just when this specific, very large generation gets old enough to need this specific kind of housing. National senior housing occupancy hit 89.5% in the first quarter of 2026, the nineteenth straight quarterly gain and the highest level since the data series began in 2006.

## 2. The supply side has basically stopped

New construction starts fell to their lowest level since 2009, and units under construction sat at their lowest level since 2012. Developers can't make the economics pencil at current labor and material costs, so even as demand strengthens, nobody's building. Industry estimates put the shortfall at roughly 806,000 units needed by 2030, and the typical project now takes about 29 months from groundbreaking to open, so even a rebound in starts this year wouldn't meaningfully add supply before 2028.

## 3. Capital has already started moving

Cap rates compressed to roughly 6.2% by year-end 2025, transaction volume hit its highest level since 2015, and a JLL survey found the largest share of institutional investors on record planning to increase senior housing allocations this year. Financing has loosened too: agency loan-purchase caps went up for 2026, and HUD's Section 232 lending program saw a sharp jump in loan volume.

## 4. Why this beats the AI trade, structurally

The AI infrastructure trend is a bet on a demand curve that could still bend, technology cycles move fast, and a data center is a depreciating, highly specialized asset if the thesis is wrong. Senior housing demand is locked in by a demographic that already exists and is already aging. The risk isn't whether people will need the housing, it's execution: labor costs, operations, and picking submarkets with real in-migration rather than markets that are already priced for perfection.

## The takeaway

I built a full underwriting exercise around this thesis (an independent living community in Frisco, TX) because I wanted to practice underwriting a real, current trend instead of a generic deal. It's over on the [portfolio page](/portfolio) if you want to see the numbers. The short version: I'd rather underwrite a demand curve that's already locked in than one that's still a bet.
