Every headline right now seems to be some version of AI this, AI that, whether it's Anthropic, OpenAI, or the hyperscalers spending hundreds of billions on infrastructure. Lately three separate threads of that story have been pulling at me at once: reporting on China's data center buildout, the rise of cheaper Chinese models like Moonshot's Kimi and DeepSeek, and enterprises actually cutting back on AI spend because the bills got too big. Individually those are technology stories. Together, they start to look like a real estate story, because data centers are buildings, and the economics of what gets built and where depends on how this all shakes out.

## Thread one: the buildout is real and still accelerating

China's installed data center capacity is projected to nearly double by 2030, adding roughly 28 gigawatts of new projects on top of the 32 gigawatts already online at the end of 2025. That is expected to more than double the sector's power consumption to around 289 terawatt-hours by 2030, growing at an annual rate near 19%. Globally, the United States and China together are projected to drive close to 80% of all data center electricity demand growth through 2030, with China's consumption rising by an estimated 170% and the US by about 130%. This is not a speculative trend. It is one of the fastest-growing sources of power demand in either country.

## Thread two: a price war that's rewriting who runs the workloads

At the same time, a handful of Chinese labs have made frontier-adjacent AI dramatically cheaper. Moonshot's Kimi K2.6 runs around $0.60 to $0.95 per million input tokens, roughly 8 to 10 times cheaper than Claude Opus on comparable workloads. DeepSeek's V4 Flash reportedly prices as low as $0.14 per million tokens against OpenAI's GPT-5.5 at $5.00, a gap of over 30x. The effect on actual usage has been fast and large. On OpenRouter, a neutral marketplace that tracks real production token volume rather than marketing claims, the combined share of US models fell from roughly 70% in June 2025 to around 30% a year later, with Chinese models from DeepSeek, Moonshot, Zhipu, and others now processing the majority of tokens on the platform. Startups have reported saving 90% on inference costs simply by switching providers.

## Thread three: some companies are pulling back hard

The flip side of "AI is everywhere" is that some of the biggest AI spenders are now actively rationing usage. Uber reportedly burned through its entire annual AI tooling budget in about four months. Microsoft canceled a large share of its direct Claude Code licenses after usage outgrew what the company considered justifiable relative to the cost. The shift to per-token, metered billing has turned AI spend into a line item CFOs can actually see and cut, which is a meaningfully different dynamic than the flat-subscription era.

## Why these three threads don't cancel each other out

It would be easy to read thread three as evidence that AI demand, and by extension data center demand, is cooling off. I don't think that holds up. Goldman Sachs is forecasting a roughly 24-fold increase in monthly token consumption by 2030, driven mostly by agentic workflows that make dozens of model calls per task instead of one. Alphabet has said it cut its Gemini serving costs by 78% through efficiency gains, and its infrastructure spending is still rising. That combination, falling unit costs alongside rising total consumption, is a classic Jevons Paradox pattern: when something gets cheaper, people don't use less of it, they find more uses for it. Cheaper tokens seem to be accelerating total compute demand, not shrinking it.

## What this actually means for CRE

Put the three threads together and the picture for real estate is more nuanced than a straight-line growth story, which is exactly why it's worth underwriting carefully rather than assuming the trend line continues untouched.

**Absolute demand for data center capacity is still going up, in both countries.** The price war and the cost-cutting stories are about who runs the workload and how efficiently, not whether the workload exists. That keeps pressure on land, power, and construction in the specialized industrial subsector that houses this infrastructure.

**But not every gigawatt announced becomes a stabilized, income-producing asset.** China's own experience is the cautionary tale here. Reporting has documented real overbuilding, with some data center expansions paused after utilization rates came in low, and Beijing has introduced subsidies specifically to boost usage of underutilized capacity. That is the CRE lesson in miniature: a capacity announcement is not a lease, and speculative development in a hot sector can outrun actual tenant demand just as easily in data centers as it can in any other property type.

**The price war could reshape where and how these facilities get built.** If Chinese models continue winning token share globally on cost, that reinforces the case for data center investment concentrated in and around China's cheaper energy markets, while US facilities may increasingly need to justify their premium through capability rather than price. That has real implications for where global capital flows in this asset class over the next few years.

The headline version of this story is "AI needs infinite data centers." The more accurate version, at least based on what's actually showing up in usage and spending data right now, is that demand is still growing fast, but it is growing unevenly, it is price sensitive, and some of the capacity being built on optimistic assumptions may not stabilize the way its investors expect. That is a familiar shape for anyone who underwrites real estate for a living.
