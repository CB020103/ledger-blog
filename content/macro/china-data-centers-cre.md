Every headline right now seems to be some version of AI this, AI that, whether it's Anthropic, OpenAI, or the hyperscalers spending hundreds of billions on infrastructure. Lately the same story has started showing up with a Chinese accent: reporting on Chinese data center capacity, power buildout, and AI infrastructure ambitions. It got me wondering what happens to real estate, both in China and by extension globally, if that buildout actually plays out at scale.

## The scale of what's being discussed

China's installed data center capacity is projected to nearly double by 2030, adding close to 28 gigawatts of new projects on top of roughly 32 gigawatts already online. That expansion is expected to more than double the sector's power consumption, driven overwhelmingly by AI and high-performance computing workloads. Globally, the United States and China together are expected to account for close to 80% of all data center electricity demand growth through 2030, with China's consumption rising by an estimated 170% over 2024 levels.

That is not a small, speculative trend. It is one of the fastest-growing sources of power demand in the country.

## Why this matters for CRE, not just chips

The obvious read is that this is an energy and technology story. But data centers are buildings, and buildings sit on land, draw on local power grids, and compete with other asset classes for construction labor, materials, and capital. A few ways this could filter into CRE if the buildout continues at pace:

**Industrial and specialized real estate absorbs the growth.** Data centers already function as a distinct, high-demand subsector of industrial real estate in the US. If China scales similarly, expect land near power infrastructure and fiber corridors to get repriced, the same way it has around US hyperscale hubs.

**Power becomes the scarce input, not land.** Reporting on the sector notes that operators are increasingly bypassing overloaded grids, and that China has moderated some of its ambitions after over-building in areas with weak actual utilization. That is a useful signal: capacity announcements do not always translate into stabilized, income-producing assets. Some Chinese data center expansions have already been paused where utilization rates came in low, which is worth remembering before assuming every gigawatt announced becomes a leasable asset.

**Capital competition, not just capacity competition.** Hyperscaler capital spending on AI infrastructure in the US alone is projected in the hundreds of billions this year. If China's buildout scales in parallel, that is a meaningful amount of global capital being pulled toward one asset type, in two countries, at the same time. For anyone underwriting industrial or specialized real estate elsewhere, that is a capital flow worth watching even if you never touch a data center deal directly.

## The honest caveat

Not everything China announces gets built, and not everything built gets used. Some of the sourcing on this points to overbuilding, transmission bottlenecks, and underutilized capacity as real problems in the current buildout, not just risks. So the more interesting question isn't whether China is building data centers. It's whether the ones being built are actually stabilizing into productive, income-generating assets, or sitting closer to speculative capacity nobody is using yet. That distinction is exactly the kind of thing CRE underwriting is built to catch, even in a sector as new as this one.
