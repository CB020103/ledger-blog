I wanted to practice underwriting something tied to a real, current trend instead of just another generic multifamily deal — so I looked at two of the biggest stories in commercial real estate right now: AI/data-center infrastructure and senior housing. Data centers are drawing enormous capital on the strength of a demand forecast, but the development pipeline in several markets is already outrunning near-term absorption. Senior housing is the opposite shape: the demand isn't a forecast, it's demographic arithmetic, and supply is years behind it.

The numbers back it up. National senior housing occupancy hit its highest level since the data series began in 2006, cap rates have compressed as institutional capital piles in, and construction starts have fallen to their lowest level in over a decade because developers can't make the economics pencil at current labor and material costs. Meanwhile the oldest baby boomers turn 80 this year — the age senior housing usage really starts to climb. Supply is structurally stuck right as demand accelerates.

For the deal itself, I picked independent living specifically because it sits closest to conventional multifamily operationally — no licensure or staffing-ratio complexity like assisted living or skilled nursing — while still capturing the service-revenue upside (dining, programming, ancillary fees) that makes it a different underwrite than a standard apartment deal. Frisco, in the Dallas–Fort Worth MSA, gives it a Sun Belt growth market with strong in-migration, without the already-priced-in premium of a gateway metro like Boston or San Francisco.

Underwritten at a 6.20% going-in cap rate with conservative 25 bps of exit cap expansion, the deal underwrites to a 15.3% levered IRR and a 1.95x equity multiple over a five-year hold. The biggest risk in the model isn't the thesis, it's the expense side — payroll and dining are the largest line items and the most exposed to labor cost inflation, which is why I underwrote expense growth above general CPI.
